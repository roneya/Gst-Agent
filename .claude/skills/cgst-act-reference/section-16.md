# Section 16 — Eligibility and Conditions for Taking Input Tax Credit

## Plain-English Summary

Section 16 is the **gateway provision for ITC**. It defines who can claim ITC, under what conditions, and within what time limit. Every ITC dispute — whether in a §61 scrutiny, §73 demand, or §74 fraud case — ultimately traces back to this section.

The section has 4 key parts:
1. **§16(1)** — Basic entitlement: registered person, business use, credited to e-credit ledger
2. **§16(2)** — 5 mandatory conditions that ALL must be satisfied simultaneously
3. **§16(3)** — No ITC if Income Tax depreciation claimed on tax component
4. **§16(4)** — Time limit to claim ITC (30th November of next FY or annual return, whichever earlier)
5. **§16(5) & (6)** — Special amnesty / revocation cases

---

## §16(1) — Basic Entitlement

**Who**: Every **registered** person
**What**: Credit of **input tax** charged on any supply of goods or services or both
**Condition**: Goods/services used or **intended to be used** in the **course or furtherance of business**
**Where**: Credited to the **electronic credit ledger**

### Key interpretive points on §16(1)

**"Intended to be used"** — ITC is available even if the goods/services have not yet been used, as long as the intention is business use. Important for capital goods and pre-operative expenses.

**"Course or furtherance of business"** — Wide test. Covers goods/services used directly or indirectly in the business. Does not require a direct nexus to the output supply.

**"Input tax"** — Means IGST, CGST, SGST/UTGST charged on the supply. Does not include GST paid on exempt supplies or non-business use (see §17 for apportionment).

---

## §16(2) — The 5 Conditions (All Must Be Satisfied)

These are cumulative — failure of any ONE condition disentitles the ITC claim. Officers raise each of these in notices.

---

### Condition (a) — Tax Invoice or Prescribed Document
The registered person must possess:
- A **tax invoice** issued by a GST-registered supplier, OR
- A **debit note** issued by such supplier, OR
- Other **prescribed tax-paying documents** (e.g., Bill of Entry for imports, ISD invoice, self-invoice for RCM)

**Defence when officer raises this**:
- Produce the original invoice
- If invoice is lost: produce duplicate invoice from supplier + affidavit
- If invoice has minor errors (wrong GSTIN, wrong address): cite CBIC Circular 26/26/2017-GST — minor errors that don't affect tax liability do not invalidate ITC
- If invoice is in a foreign currency: converted value at customs rate on date of supply

---

### Condition (aa) — Supplier Has Filed GSTR-1 (Inserted w.e.f. 01.01.2022)
The details of the invoice/debit note must have been:
- **Furnished by the supplier** in their outward supplies statement (GSTR-1), AND
- **Communicated to the recipient** under §37 (i.e., visible in GSTR-2B of the recipient)

**This is the GSTR-2A/2B mismatch ground** — the most common allegation in GST notices today.

**Defence**:
- (aa) was inserted only w.e.f. 01.01.2022 — for periods before that date, it does not apply
- Even post-01.01.2022: multiple High Courts have held that GSTR-2B mismatch alone is not grounds to deny ITC if the taxpayer can prove genuineness of the transaction through: invoice, e-way bill, proof of payment, proof of receipt of goods/services, supplier's GSTIN being active at the time
- The department cannot deny ITC to a bonafide recipient for the supplier's default in filing GSTR-1
- Rule 36(4) restricts ITC to 105%/110% of GSTR-2B (for relevant periods) — but a bonafide claim can be argued
- Always check: was the supplier's GSTIN active? Was tax actually deposited by the supplier?

---

### Condition (b) — Receipt of Goods or Services
The recipient must have **actually received** the goods or services.

**Deemed receipt (Explanation)**:
- Goods: deemed received when delivered to recipient or any other person **on the direction** of the registered person (covers bill-to-ship-to transactions, agent deliveries)
- Services: deemed received when provided to any person **on the direction of and on account of** the registered person

**Defence when officer alleges non-receipt**:
- Produce: delivery challans, lorry receipts, e-way bills, goods receipt notes (GRN), stock records
- For services: work orders, completion certificates, service reports, bank payment records
- For bill-to-ship-to: show the direction/instruction given to supplier; e-way bill with correct ship-to address

---

### Condition (ba) — ITC Not Restricted Under §38 (Inserted w.e.f. 01.01.2022)
The ITC communicated to the recipient under §38 should not have been **restricted**.

§38 allows the system to restrict ITC in GSTR-2B based on risky supplier parameters. If the system has restricted ITC, the recipient cannot claim it until the restriction is lifted.

**Defence**:
- Apply to the department to lift the restriction on the supplier's GSTIN
- If restriction is wrongly applied: file grievance on GST portal; approach Appellate Authority
- (ba) is system-driven — the recipient has limited control; flag as administrative issue, not recipient's fault

---

### Condition (c) — Tax Actually Paid to Government
The tax charged on the supply must have been **actually paid to the Government** — either in cash or through ITC utilisation by the supplier.

**This is the supplier default ground** — officer alleges the supplier did not deposit the tax despite collecting it from the buyer.

**Defence (most important — consistently upheld by courts)**:
- The recipient cannot be penalised for the supplier's default in paying tax, provided the recipient acted in good faith
- The recipient's obligation is to pay the supplier (including GST); the supplier's obligation to deposit tax is between the supplier and the government
- Always produce: bank payment proof showing GST paid to supplier, supplier's GSTIN validity at time of transaction
- If the supplier is a fake/non-existent entity or if there is collusion, this defence weakens — the transaction itself must be genuine

---

### Condition (d) — Return Filed Under §39
The recipient must have **filed their GSTR-3B** for the relevant period.

**Defence**:
- If return was filed late: ITC is still available (late filing ≠ ineligibility); only late fee applies
- If return was not filed: ITC cannot be claimed for that period until the return is filed
- Check if the return for the relevant period is now filed — if yes, ITC becomes available from the date of filing

---

## The 180-Day Rule — Second Proviso to §16(2) ⭐ Very Common Allegation

**The Rule**: If the recipient **fails to pay the supplier** (value + tax) within **180 days** from the date of the invoice, the ITC availed must be **reversed** along with **interest under §50**.

**Exception**: Does not apply to supplies under **reverse charge (RCM)**.

**Re-availment**: Once the recipient actually pays the supplier, the ITC can be **re-availed** (third proviso).

### How to handle 180-day notices

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Check if payment was made within 180 days — bank statements, RTGS/NEFT records |
| 2 | Check if payment was partial — reversal is proportionate to unpaid portion |
| 3 | Check if RCM applies — if yes, 180-day rule does not apply at all |
| 4 | If reversal is due, was it done in GSTR-3B Table 4(B)(2) for the period 180 days expired? |
| 5 | If payment was made later, was ITC re-availed in the return for the month of payment? |

**Interest on reversal**: 18% p.a. from the date ITC was availed to the date of reversal.

**Defence**:
- Produce bank records showing payment within 180 days
- If payment made in instalments: show instalment dates and amounts
- Argue: rule applies only where the recipient has **not paid at all** — partial non-payment triggers proportionate reversal only

## §16(3) — No ITC if Depreciation Claimed on Tax Component

If the taxpayer has claimed **depreciation under Income Tax Act** on the **GST component** of capital goods / plant & machinery — ITC is not allowed on that GST amount.

**Defence**: Produce asset register and IT depreciation workings showing GST was **excluded** from the depreciable cost (standard accounting treatment: GST claimed as ITC, not capitalized).

---

## §16(4) — Time Limit to Claim ITC ⭐ Critical

ITC must be claimed by the **earlier** of:
- **30th November** following the end of the FY to which the invoice pertains, OR
- The date of furnishing the **relevant GSTR-9 (annual return)**

| Invoice FY | Last date to claim ITC |
|---|---|
| FY 2021-22 | 30-Nov-2022 or GSTR-9 filing date, whichever earlier |
| FY 2022-23 | 30-Nov-2023 or GSTR-9 filing date, whichever earlier |
| FY 2023-24 | 30-Nov-2024 or GSTR-9 filing date, whichever earlier |
| FY 2024-25 | 30-Nov-2025 or GSTR-9 filing date, whichever earlier |

**Hard cutoff** — ITC claimed after this date is time-barred even if all other §16(2) conditions are met.

**Defence when §16(4) is raised**:
- Check: when was the ITC entry made in GSTR-3B? (Not invoice date — GSTR-3B filing date)
- For FY 2017-18 to 2020-21: check §16(5) amnesty below
- Revocation cases: check §16(6) below
- Constitutional challenge: some HCs have upheld §16(4) — but petitions are pending; flag to client

---

## §16(5) — Amnesty for FY 2017-18 to FY 2020-21

For invoices pertaining to **FY 2017-18, 2018-19, 2019-20, and 2020-21**, ITC could be claimed in any GSTR-3B filed **up to 30th November 2021**.

**This amnesty has expired.** Verify filing date from GST portal — return must have been filed by 30-Nov-2021.

---

## §16(6) — ITC After Revocation of Cancelled Registration

Where registration was cancelled and subsequently revoked, the taxpayer can claim ITC for the cancellation period by filing a return:
- **(i)** By 30th November of the relevant FY (or GSTR-9, whichever earlier), OR
- **(ii)** Within **30 days** of the revocation order — whichever is **later**

## Key Case Law

### GSTR-2A mismatch — bonafide recipient protected
**Hindustan Herbal Cosmetics v. State of U.P. (Allahabad HC, 2022)**
> ITC cannot be denied to a bonafide purchaser merely on account of GSTR-2A mismatch, if the purchaser has valid invoices, has actually received goods, and has paid the supplier including tax. Department must first proceed against the defaulting supplier.

**D.Y. Beathel Enterprises v. State Tax Officer (Madras HC, 2021)**
> The department cannot proceed against the purchasing dealer for ITC recovery when the supplier failed to remit the tax. The purchasing dealer who paid the supplier (including tax) is not at fault.

### Supplier tax default — recipient not liable
**Arising Food Industries v. Commissioner (Kerala HC)**
> The liability to deposit tax is on the supplier. A bonafide recipient who purchased goods, received them, and paid the supplier cannot bear the consequences of the supplier's failure to deposit tax.

### §16(4) — time limit is mandatory
**Filco Trade Centre Pvt. Ltd. v. Union of India (Gujarat HC)**
> The time limit under §16(4) is a substantive condition, not a procedural formality. ITC claimed beyond the time limit is not sustainable even if all other conditions are met.

### Condition (aa) — applicable only from 01.01.2022
> Condition (aa) was inserted w.e.f. 01.01.2022. For invoices before that date, GSTR-2A/2B mismatch alone cannot be the basis for ITC denial.
