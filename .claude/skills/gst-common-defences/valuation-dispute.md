# Valuation Disputes — Defence Playbook

## What Is a Valuation Dispute?

A valuation dispute arises when the proper officer **rejects the transaction value declared by the taxpayer** and substitutes a higher value for GST purposes, thereby demanding additional tax on the difference.

- Governed by: **Section 15** of CGST Act, 2017
- Rules: **CGST Valuation Rules (Rules 27–35)**

---

## Section 15 — The Basic Rule

**Transaction value** = the price actually paid or payable for the supply between unrelated parties **+ inclusions − exclusions**.

**Section 15(1):** Transaction value is accepted as the taxable value IF:
- Supplier and recipient are not related
- Price is the sole consideration

**Section 15(2) — Inclusions (add to value):**
- Any taxes, duties (other than GST itself)
- Amount of interest, late fee, penalty for delayed payment
- Subsidies linked to the supply (from buyer or any person)
- Other incidental expenses (packing, commission, etc.)

**Section 15(3) — Exclusions (deduct from value):**
- Discounts given before/at time of supply (if on invoice)
- Post-supply discounts if: pre-agreed in contract + ITC reversed by recipient

## Common Valuation SCN Grounds and Defences

### Ground 1 — Related Party Transaction Revaluation

**Officer's allegation:** Supplier and recipient are related (same group, common directors); transaction value is not arm's length; higher value should apply.

**Defence:**
- Prove the relationship does not affect the price — price is at market rates
- Show comparable market prices for the same goods/services from unrelated parties
- If value is genuinely at arm's length → officer cannot substitute a different value merely because parties are related
- Rule 28 of Valuation Rules: If related party transaction — use open market value. If open market value is not available — cost-plus 10% method applies.

*Cite: Open market value must be for identical goods in the ordinary course of trade*

---

### Ground 2 — Free Samples / Free Supplies

**Officer's allegation:** Goods supplied free of cost; tax should be on the market value.

**Defence:**
- Section 17(5)(h) bars ITC on goods given as gifts/free samples
- If ITC on the inputs was reversed (as required) → no tax on the outward free supply
- Rule 28 provides for nil value if full ITC is blocked at input stage
- Many HCs have held: when input ITC is reversed, no additional tax on the free supply output

---

### Ground 3 — Discount Not Accepted

**Officer's allegation:** Discount given to customer is not deductible; full MRP is the taxable value.

**Defence:**
- **Pre-supply discount (on invoice):** Fully deductible under §15(3)(a) if on the invoice itself — no conditions
- **Post-supply discount:** Deductible if:
  1. Pre-agreed in writing (contract / scheme)
  2. Linked to sales performance / volume
  3. Recipient has reversed the ITC attributable to the discount

Show the discount agreement / scheme document + ITC reversal by recipient (credit note issued + ITC reversal intimation).

---

### Ground 4 — Recharacterisation as Sale (Instead of Job Work / Lease)

**Officer's allegation:** What you call job work / lease is actually a sale; GST should be charged on full value.

**Defence:**
- Produce the contract / agreement showing the nature of transaction (job work / lease)
- Show physical return of goods after job work (challan, return records)
- For lease: Show the goods remain with the lessor / return at lease end
- The substance and legal form of the transaction determine GST treatment — not the officer's recharacterisation

---

### Ground 5 — Subsidy / Third-Party Payment

**Officer's allegation:** A third party pays part of the consideration; the full value (including third-party payment) should be the taxable value.

**Defence:**
- §15(2)(e) includes third-party consideration only if it is "linked to the supply"
- Government subsidies are specifically excluded from taxable value (§15(2)(e) proviso)
- Identify whether the subsidy/third-party payment is linked to the price or is an unrelated grant

---

### Ground 6 — Inclusions Missed — Interest / Penalty Charges

**Officer's allegation:** Interest charged on delayed payment by customer should be included in taxable value.

**Defence:**
- §15(2)(d) includes interest/penalty **for delayed payment of consideration** in taxable value
- However, if the interest is for financing (distinct financial service) → not taxable under the main supply
- Check if interest is separately invoiced by a financing entity (not the supplier) → separate supply, separate valuation

## Valuation Rules Quick Reference (Rules 27–35)

| Rule | Method | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| Rule 27 | Open market value | Related parties — if OMV available |
| Rule 28 | Value of like kind and quality | Related parties — no OMV |
| Rule 29 | Cost-based method | When other methods unavailable |
| Rule 30 | Residual method | Last resort |
| Rule 32 | Special cases | Money changers, air travel agents, insurance |
| Rule 33 | Pure agent | Third-party payments excluded |
| Rule 35 | Tax exclusive value | Working back from tax-inclusive price |

---

## Key Legal Points

1. **Transaction value is the starting point** — Officer cannot reject it without positive evidence that it does not represent arm's length value.

2. **Burden of proof** — Officer must establish that the declared value is not the correct value; taxpayer does not have to prove a negative.

3. **Rule 33 — Pure Agent** — Amounts paid by taxpayer as pure agent on behalf of recipient are excluded from taxable value (e.g., customs duty, government fees paid on client's behalf).

4. **GST ≠ Customs Valuation** — GST valuation rules are different from customs; do not confuse the two.

## Key Case Law

- *CCE v. Bata India Ltd* — Discounts given in ordinary course of trade are deductible
- *Imagic Creative Pvt Ltd* (SC) — Related party valuation — OMV must be for identical goods
- *DSCL Sugar Ltd* — Subsidy by state government not includible in transaction value
- Multiple AARs on free samples, promotional supplies, and buyback transactions
