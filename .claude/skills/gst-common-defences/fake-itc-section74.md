# Fake ITC / Section 74 Fraud Allegation — Defence Playbook

## What Is This SCN About?

When the officer alleges that ITC was claimed on **fake invoices** (invoices without actual supply of goods/services), the SCN is typically issued under **Section 74** (fraud / willful misstatement / suppression of facts) — which carries:
- 5-year limitation (instead of 3 years under §73)
- Penalty up to 100% of tax (instead of 10% under §73)

This is the most serious GST allegation a taxpayer can face.

---

## What the Officer Must Prove (Burden of Proof)

Section 74 applies **only** when the officer can establish:
1. **Fraud** — deliberate deception
2. **Willful misstatement** — knowingly stating incorrect facts
3. **Suppression of facts** — intentional concealment of material information

> **Critical point:** The burden of proof lies on the REVENUE, not the taxpayer. Mere mismatch in returns or GSTIN cancellation of a supplier does NOT automatically establish fraud.

*Cite: Section 74(1) — "reasons to believe that any tax has not been paid... by reason of fraud, or any willful-misstatement or suppression of facts"*

## Common Scenarios and Defences

### Scenario 1 — Supplier's GSTIN Cancelled Retrospectively
**Officer's allegation:** Your supplier's registration was cancelled retrospectively; therefore, supplies received were invalid.

**Defence:**
- Retrospective cancellation cannot invalidate bona fide transactions done during the period the supplier was apparently registered
- The taxpayer had no knowledge of the future cancellation
- The taxpayer relied on the supplier's valid GSTIN at the time of supply
- Section 16(2) conditions (receipt of goods, tax invoice, payment to supplier) were met
- The taxpayer is a victim, not a conspirator

*Cite: Multiple HC decisions — retrospective cancellation cannot affect bona fide purchasers*

---

### Scenario 2 — Supplier Found to be Non-Existent / Shell Entity
**Officer's allegation:** Supplier was a fictitious entity, no actual supply occurred.

**Defence:**
- **Prove actual receipt of goods/services:**
  - Purchase orders + delivery challans
  - Stock records showing receipt and consumption
  - E-way bills accompanying the goods
  - Transport / logistics documents
  - Quality inspection records
  - Storage records showing the goods were physically received
- **Prove actual payment to supplier:**
  - Bank transfer receipts (NEFT/RTGS/IMPS — no cash payments)
  - Supplier's bank account confirmation
- **Supplier's compliance at time of transaction:**
  - Supplier was registered and filed returns for the relevant period
  - GSTR-1 was filed by supplier showing these invoices
  - Supplier appeared in GSTR-2A / GSTR-2B

---

### Scenario 3 — DGGI Investigation — Supplier Network Bust
**Officer's allegation:** Your supplier is part of a fake invoice network busted by DGGI.

**Defence:**
- The taxpayer is a bona fide purchaser with no knowledge of the supplier's other activities
- The taxpayer conducted due diligence — verified GSTIN, received goods, paid through banking channels
- The criminal acts (if any) were by the supplier, not the taxpayer
- ITC cannot be denied to an innocent purchaser for supplier's default
- The investigation against the supplier is a separate matter — it does not automatically make the taxpayer's ITC ineligible

*Cite: Arise India Ltd v. CTO (Del HC) — ITC cannot be denied to buyer for supplier's fraud without evidence of buyer's complicity*

---

### Scenario 4 — Goods Not Actually Received (Officer's Claim)
**Officer's allegation:** Despite invoice, goods were never actually received.

**Defence:**
- Present the full documentary chain:
  1. Purchase order
  2. Supplier's invoice
  3. E-way bill (with vehicle number, transporter)
  4. Goods receipt note / delivery challan
  5. Entry in stock / inventory register
  6. Consumption records / production records showing goods used
  7. Payment proof (bank transfer)
- If officer disputes any document → ask for specific reason and respond document by document

## Key Legal Argument — §74 Cannot Be Invoked for Mismatch Alone

A mere difference between GSTR-2A/2B and GSTR-3B, or a supplier's cancellation, does NOT constitute fraud. The officer must produce positive evidence of:
- The taxpayer's knowledge of the supplier's fictitious nature
- Collusion between taxpayer and supplier
- No actual supply of goods/services

Without such evidence, the correct section is §73 (non-fraud) — not §74.

**Prayer in DRC-06:** *"The allegations of fraud / willful misstatement / suppression are denied. The Revenue has failed to produce any material to establish the mens rea required under §74. The invocation of §74 instead of §73 is without basis and the proceedings are liable to be converted to §73 with the lower penalty and 3-year limitation applicable thereto."*

## Due Diligence Checklist (For Future Protection)

For every new supplier, document:
- [ ] Supplier GSTIN verified on GST portal (screenshot with date)
- [ ] Supplier's return filing status checked
- [ ] Physical existence verified (visit / Google Maps / geo-tagged photo)
- [ ] Payment only through banking channels (no cash)
- [ ] E-way bill for every consignment
- [ ] Signed delivery challan / GRN

---

## Key Case Law

- *Arise India Ltd v. CTO* (Del HC) — ITC cannot be denied to bona fide buyer without proving complicity
- *Suncraft Energy Pvt Ltd* (Cal HC) — Supplier's GSTR-2A non-reflection not conclusive proof of fake supply
- *Devi Dass Goyal v. State of Haryana* — Retrospective cancellation of supplier does not invalidate buyer's ITC
- *M/s Vimal Petrofils* (Guj HC) — Burden of proving fraud is on Revenue
- *JSW Steel Ltd* — Section 74 invocation requires specific evidence of fraud, not presumption
