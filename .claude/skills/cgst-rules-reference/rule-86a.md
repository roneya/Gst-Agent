# Rule 86A — Conditions for Use of Amount Available in Electronic Credit Ledger

## What Is Rule 86A?

Rule 86A gives the proper officer power to **block the electronic credit ledger (ITC)** of a registered person if the officer has reasons to believe that the ITC was fraudulently availed or is ineligible.

This is one of the most aggressive actions available to GST officers — it freezes the taxpayer's ITC immediately, disrupting business operations.

---

## When Can the Officer Invoke Rule 86A?

The officer must have **reasons to believe** that credit has been availed:
1. On the strength of invoices/debit notes issued by a non-existent registered person
2. On the strength of invoices where no supply has actually been taken place
3. On invoices where tax has not been paid by the supplier to the government
4. On invoices issued by a person whose registration has been cancelled retrospectively

---

## Key Features of Rule 86A Blocking

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Authority | Commissioner or officer authorised by Commissioner |
| Duration | **Maximum 1 year** from date of blocking |
| Prior notice | NOT required — can be done without SCN |
| Effect | ITC in credit ledger cannot be used for payment of output tax |
| Cash ledger | Unaffected — cash deposits still usable |

---

## Immediate Steps on Receiving Rule 86A Block Notice

1. **Check portal immediately** — Electronic credit ledger will show "blocked" status
2. **Note the date of blocking** — 1-year limit runs from this date
3. **Identify the grounds stated** — Officer must record reasons in writing
4. **Assess impact on business** — Can output tax be paid from cash ledger temporarily?
5. **Engage CA / advocate immediately** — Rule 86A challenge is time-sensitive

---

## Legal Grounds to Challenge Rule 86A Blocking

### Ground 1 — No Written Reasons Recorded
Rule 86A requires the officer to have "reasons to believe" — this must be reduced to writing. If no written reasons are available, the blocking is procedurally invalid.

*Cite: Multiple HC decisions holding that "reasons to believe" must be recorded and communicated*

### Ground 2 — Blocking Without SCN / Opportunity
The Supreme Court and various HCs have held that although Rule 86A does not expressly require prior notice, principles of natural justice require an opportunity **before** blocking (or at minimum immediately after).

*Cite: Amit Kumar Gupta v. Union of India; Aggarwal Dyeing & Printing Works v. State of Gujarat (Guj HC)*

### Ground 3 — Supplier Has Paid Tax
If the supplier has actually paid the tax to the government (verifiable from GSTR-3B), the ground for blocking (non-payment by supplier) does not exist.

### Ground 4 — Supplies Actually Received
If the taxpayer can demonstrate actual receipt of goods/services (e-way bills, delivery challans, payment records, bank transfers), the "no supply" ground fails.

### Ground 5 — 1-Year Period Expired
After 1 year from the date of blocking, the credit ledger must be automatically unblocked. If not unblocked → writ petition / representation to Commissioner.

### Ground 6 — Blocking Exceeds ITC Actually Availed
The officer cannot block more ITC than the amount allegedly wrongly availed. If the block exceeds the alleged ITC → challenge the excess blocking.

## How to Get ITC Unblocked

### Step 1 — File Written Representation to the Officer
Within 7 days of blocking, file a detailed representation covering:
- Business impact
- Proof of genuine supplies (invoices, e-way bills, payments)
- Supplier's GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B showing tax paid
- Legal grounds challenging the blocking

### Step 2 — Request Order Under Rule 86A(2)
If the officer is satisfied that conditions for blocking no longer exist, they shall **unblock the credit within 1 year** under Rule 86A(2).

### Step 3 — Writ Petition (High Court)
If the officer refuses to unblock:
- File a writ petition under Article 226 of the Constitution
- Seek stay of the blocking order
- Most HCs have granted interim relief where prima facie case is made out

---

## Key Differences — Rule 86A vs ITC Reversal Demand

| Aspect | Rule 86A Blocking | ITC Reversal Demand (§73/74) |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | Provisional freeze on credit | Final demand to reverse and pay |
| SCN required | No | Yes |
| Duration | Max 1 year | Permanent (if order passed) |
| Challenge | Representation + writ | DRC-06 reply + appeal |
| Effect on cash | No effect | No effect |

## Standard Representation Draft (After Rule 86A Block)

> "This is to bring to your kind notice that the electronic credit ledger of [Name], GSTIN [GSTIN] has been blocked under Rule 86A of CGST Rules, 2017 on [date]. We respectfully submit the following in support of our request for immediate restoration of the credit ledger:
>
> 1. The supplies in question were actually received by us from [Supplier Name], GSTIN [Supplier GSTIN], as evidenced by the purchase invoices, e-way bills, and delivery challans enclosed herewith.
>
> 2. The supplier has duly paid the tax in question to the Government, as evidenced by their GSTR-3B for the relevant period, a copy of which is enclosed.
>
> 3. The blocking of ITC amounting to ₹[Amount] is causing severe disruption to our business operations as we are unable to discharge our output tax liability from the credit ledger.
>
> We therefore request that the block imposed under Rule 86A be immediately lifted and our credit ledger be restored. We are available for personal discussion at your convenience."

## Key Case Law

- *Aggarwal Dyeing & Printing Works v. State of Gujarat* (Guj HC) — natural justice applies to Rule 86A
- *Samay Alloys v. State of Gujarat* (Guj HC) — blocking must be proportionate to alleged ITC
- *Amit Kumar Gupta v. UOI* — reasons to believe must be recorded
